5 Hidden Gems in Kenya You've Probably Never Heard Of

Everyone knows the Maasai Mara. Everyone knows Diani Beach. But Kenya is a country of extraordinary depth — and beyond the famous parks and coastlines lies a whole other Kenya that most travellers never find. Here are five destinations that our team returns to again and again, precisely because they remain beautifully, stubbornly off the tourist map.

1. Laikipia Plateau — Where conservancies outnumber tourists and rhinos roam freely across private wilderness.

2. Takawiri Island — A tiny island on Lake Victoria reachable only by boat, with no cars, no crowds, and some of the best freshwater fishing in East Africa.

3. Hell's Gate National Park — The only park in Kenya where you cycle and walk freely among wildlife, through dramatic gorges carved by ancient rivers.

4. Samburu National Reserve — Home to the "Samburu Special Five" — rare northern species found nowhere else in Kenya.

5. Lake Turkana — The world's largest desert lake, stretching across Kenya's remote northwest in shades of jade green and volcanic black.

Packing Smart: What to Bring on a Multi-Climate Trip

One of the most common questions we get from travellers booking our Europe Grand Tour or a combined safari-and-beach package is: how do I pack for a trip where the weather changes completely from one destination to the next? The answer is simpler than most people think — and it starts with abandoning the idea of packing for specific outfits.

The layering principle: Instead of packing separate wardrobes for different climates, build every outfit around layers that can be added or removed. A merino wool base layer works in Swiss mountain cold and Kenyan morning game drives alike.

Safari essentials: Neutral colours (khaki, olive, beige), long sleeves for sun and insects, comfortable walking shoes, and a wide-brimmed hat. Leave bright colours at home — they disturb wildlife.

Beach additions: A swimsuit, reef-safe sunscreen, a lightweight sarong, and flip flops. That's genuinely all you need.

Europe specifics: One smart-casual outfit for nicer dinners, comfortable walking shoes (you will walk 15,000+ steps a day), and a compact rain jacket.

Lamu: Kenya's Most Magical Island and Why You Need to Go Now

There are no cars on Lamu Island. This single fact — more than anything else — explains why Lamu feels so profoundly different from everywhere else in Kenya. When the only sounds are the clip-clop of donkey hooves on coral stone, the call to prayer drifting from the mosque, and the creak of dhow sails on the channel, you realise how much of modern travel is defined by noise — and how rare true quiet has become.

Getting there: A 1-hour flight from Nairobi to Lamu Airport, then a short motorised boat across the channel to Lamu town. The boat ride itself — arriving by sea — is one of the great travel arrivals in East Africa.

What to do: Get lost in the old town's labyrinthine alleys (a UNESCO World Heritage Site since 2001), take a traditional dhow sailing trip at sunset, visit the Lamu Museum, and eat the freshest seafood of your life at a waterfront restaurant.

Best time to visit: July–August for dry season, or the Lamu Cultural Festival in November — one of Kenya's most authentic annual events celebrating Swahili culture.
